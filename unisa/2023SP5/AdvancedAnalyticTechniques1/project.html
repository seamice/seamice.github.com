                                                                  # What is Kalma Filter

                                                                  The Kalman Filter is a mathematical algorithm used for estimating and predicting the state of a dynamic system, particularly in the presence of noisy or uncertain data. It consist of five equations.

                                                                  # Important basic concepts

                                                                  1. Mean μ=1N∑n=1NVn\mu = \frac{1}{N}\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N}V_nμ=N1​n=1∑N​Vn​
                                                                  2. Expectation: The mean of multiple measurements， similar with mean
                                                                  3. Variance: σ2=1N∑n=1N(Vn−μ)2\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N}(V_n-\mu)^2σ2=N1​n=1∑N​(Vn​−μ)2
                                                                  4. Standard Variance: σ\sigmaσ
                                                                  5. Normal Distribution (All called Gaussian Distribution)
                                                                    f(x;μ,σ2)=12πσ2e−(x−μ)22σ2f(x; \mu, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}e^{\frac{-(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}f(x;μ,σ2)=2πσ2​1​e2σ2−(x−μ)2​

                                                                    Typically, measurement errors are normally distributed. The Kalman filter assumes that the measurement errors have a normal distribution.
                                                                  6. Estimation: An estimate of the hidden state of the system.
                                                                  7. Accuracy: Describes how close a measured value is to the true value.
                                                                  8. Precision: Describes the deviation distribution of a series of measured values relative to the same true value

                                                                    Info

                                                                    High-precision systems have measurements with very low variance (i.e., low uncertainty), while low-precision systems have high variance (i.e., high uncertainty).

                                                                  Where ωn\omega_nωn​ is the process noise at the time step nnn.

                                                                  We've seen that the process noise variance has a critical influence on the Kalman Filter performance. Too small qqq causes a lag error (see Example 7open in new window).. If the qqq value is too high, the Kalman Filter follows the measurements (see Example 8open in new window) and produces noisy estimations.

                                                                  The process noise can be independent between different state variables. In this case, the process noise covariance matrix Qˉ\bar QQˉ​ is a diagonal matrix:

                                                                  Q=[q110…00q22…0⋮⋮⋱⋮00...qkk] Q = \begin{bmatrix} q_{11} & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & q_{22} & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & ... & q_{kk} \end{bmatrix} Q=​q11​0⋮0​0q22​⋮0​……⋱...​00⋮qkk​​​

                                                                  The process noise can also be dependent. For example, the constant velocity model assumes zero acceleration (a=0a=0a=0). However, a random variance in acceleration σ2aσ2aσ2a causes a variance in velocity and position. In this case, the process noise is correlated with the state variables.

                                                                  • Continuous noise model

                                                                  In many cases, the measured value is not the desired system state.
